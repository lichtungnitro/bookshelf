# Connecting the Dots

> "Edwin Land of Polaroid talked about the intersection of the humanities and science. I like that intersection. There's something magical about that place."
> — Steve Jobs, in his own words

> "There's a deep current of humanity in our innovation."

## The Principle

Jobs' most consequential creative acts were *importations*—taking a concept fully formed from one domain and dropping it into another where it had never existed.

He audited a calligraphy class at Reed College after dropping out. It seemed useless. A decade later, it became the reason the Macintosh had beautiful typography—the first personal computer to care about fonts. "When we were designing the first Macintosh computer, it all came back to me."

He couldn't have planned this connection. He could only have made it possible by remaining curious across domains that "serious" engineers ignored.

## The Mechanism

The connecting-dots insight requires two things:

1. **Accumulated incongruous experiences** — Things that don't obviously belong together in your field
2. **The willingness to ask "what if this worked there?"** — Applying a pattern from domain A to problem B

Jobs spent time studying Sony's product brochures, visiting the Bauhaus-influenced Aspen Institute, practicing Zen, admiring Porsche engineering, and thinking about how Tiffany glass was beautiful art produced at industrial scale. None of these had obvious application to computers. All of them shaped what Apple built.

His conviction: "Great artists like Leonardo da Vinci and Michelangelo were also great at science. Michelangelo knew a lot about how to quarry stone, not just how to be a sculptor."

The narrowly-trained specialist can execute. Only the person with wide inputs can *originate*.

## How to Apply It

**When facing a hard design or creative problem:**

1. **Name what the thing needs to *feel* like**, not what it needs to *do*.
   - Jobs didn't say "the Mac needs better UI." He said it needed to feel *friendly*, like a human face. That reframe changed the physical design.

2. **Ask: where has this feeling already been achieved?**
   - Find the field that has already solved the emotional/experiential problem, even if it's in an unrelated domain.
   - Jobs found the answer in Japanese Zen gardens, Braun industrial design, Porsche's form language.

3. **Import the solution directly.**
   - Don't adapt it gently. Bring it in fully and see what breaks. The tension is generative.

4. **"Our task is to read things that are not yet on the page."**
   - Most users cannot tell you what they want. They can only recognize it when they see it. This means the designer must synthesize across broader inputs than any user has access to.

## The Anti-Pattern

Microsoft had "salespeople running the company." Jobs' diagnosis: "Microsoft never had the humanities and liberal arts in its DNA. Even when they saw the Mac, they couldn't copy it well. They totally didn't get it."

Copying requires understanding. Understanding requires having lived in the domain that generated the original insight. A purely technical team copying a human-centered design will produce the right shapes with the wrong soul.

## When to Apply This Skill

Apply when:
- The solution to a problem exists in a different domain than where the problem lives
- A user is thinking only within their own field and producing incremental, not original, ideas
- The question is how to make something feel a certain way, not just function correctly
- A technical solution lacks human resonance

The move: name the feeling, find where it already exists in the world, import the pattern. Don't ask permission from the domain you're working in.
