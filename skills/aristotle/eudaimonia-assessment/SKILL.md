# Eudaimonia — Genuine Flourishing

> "Happiness is the activity of the soul in accordance with virtue." — Aristotle

Aristotle distinguishes *eudaimonia* — genuine human flourishing — from its common substitutes. Most people pursue happiness, but mistake the means for the end. The result is a life that accumulates goods while remaining, at its core, unsatisfied.

The examination is not about judgment. It is about clarity.

## The Three False Ends

Aristotle names three lives people mistake for the good life:

**1. The life of pleasure.**
Pleasure is real and good — but it is the *concomitant* of excellent activity, not its substance. A life organized around pleasure pursues the shadow of the good, not the good itself. It is the life of "fatted cattle."

**2. The life of honor.**
Honor depends entirely on those who bestow it. It is too external, too contingent, too vulnerable to fortune. The person whose worth depends on recognition has placed their flourishing outside themselves.

**3. The life of money-making.**
Wealth is purely instrumental — a means to other things. No one desires money for its own sake in the final sense; they desire what money enables. To pursue it as the end is to confuse a tool for a life.

## The Function Argument

Aristotle asks: what is the *ergon* — the characteristic function — of a human being? Every thing has a function: the eye sees, the knife cuts. What does a human being, as such, *do*?

His answer: to live in accordance with reason — and not merely to possess reason, but to *exercise* it excellently, in action, over a complete life.

Eudaimonia is therefore not a feeling. It is an *activity*: the ongoing exercise of distinctively human capacities — reason, virtue, relationship, contemplation — in their fullest expression.

## The Assessment

When guiding this examination, move through four areas:

**1. Activity — what are you actually doing?**
Not what you value in the abstract. What does your time, energy, and attention actually go toward? List the main activities. Which of these exercise your capacities excellently? Which are merely instrumental or habitual?

**2. Virtue — is character engaged?**
Activities that engage courage, justice, generosity, honesty, practical wisdom tend toward flourishing. Activities that require none of these — or that require their opposites — tend away from it.

**3. Completeness — is it over a whole life?**
Aristotle insists: "one swallow does not make a spring." A single excellent day, or excellent year, is not eudaimonia. The assessment must consider patterns, not episodes. Is this life, as it is going, one that is becoming more fully itself?

**4. External goods — what does fortune require?**
Aristotle is honest: eudaimonia is easier with health, friendship, adequate resources, and reasonable luck. It is not impossible without them, but more difficult. Do not dismiss the material and relational conditions of a flourishing life as if they were irrelevant.

## What to Produce

After the assessment, offer a clear, honest summary:

- Where the user's life is most aligned with genuine flourishing
- Where it is most misaligned — and toward which false end the misalignment tends
- One concrete shift in activity or orientation that moves toward the mean

Avoid vague encouragement. Aristotle's philosophy is practical and honest. The good life requires not just insight but action — and action sustained over time.

> "It is not enough to know virtue; we must endeavour to possess it, and use it, or to take any other steps that may make us good." — Aristotle
