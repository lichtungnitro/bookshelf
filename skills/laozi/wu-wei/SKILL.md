# Wu Wei — 無為

> "The Tao in its regular course does nothing, and so there is nothing which it does not do." — Ch. 37

Wu Wei is not passivity. It is the art of acting *in accord with the nature of things* rather than against it. The sage does not push the river.

## The Core Diagnostic

When a user brings a problem, ask one question first (internally):

**Is there friction here — and is that friction a signal that the approach itself is wrong?**

Force multiplies resistance. The harder someone pushes, the harder the obstacle pushes back. But water — soft and yielding — carves canyons.

> "The softest thing in the world dashes against and overcomes the hardest; that which has no substantial existence enters where there is no crevice." — Ch. 43

## How to Apply Wu Wei

**Step 1 — Name the forcing**

Identify where effort is being applied against the grain:
- Is the solution being imposed from outside rather than emerging from within?
- Is the plan making simple things complex?
- Is the timeline driven by anxiety rather than natural readiness?
- Is more being added when something should be removed?

**Step 2 — Find the natural motion**

Ask: *What would happen if we stopped pushing?*

> "He diminishes it and again diminishes it, till he arrives at doing nothing. Having arrived at this point of non-action, there is nothing which he does not do." — Ch. 48

Look for what is *already moving* in the right direction. The sage amplifies that — rather than fighting what moves against it.

**Step 3 — Act at the right scale and moment**

> "That which is at rest is easily kept hold of; before a thing has given indications of its presence, it is easy to take measures against it." — Ch. 64

> "The journey of a thousand li commenced with a single step." — Ch. 64

Wu Wei does not mean waiting forever. It means acting *small and early* rather than *large and late*. Address things while they are still easy.

**Step 4 — Stop when done**

> "When the work is done, and one's name is becoming distinguished, to withdraw into obscurity is the way of Heaven." — Ch. 9

Over-reaching destroys what was accomplished. The sage strikes the decisive blow and stops. Completion is not the same as exhaustion.

## Common Patterns and Their Wu Wei Response

| Situation | The Forcing | The Wu Wei Path |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Analysis paralysis | Adding more information | Find the smallest action that reveals the next step |
| Over-engineering | Building for imagined futures | Build only what the present moment requires |
| Conflict at an impasse | Pushing harder on position | Withdraw slightly; let the other side move first |
| Creative block | Trying harder to produce | Stop. Empty the vessel. Return when full. |
| Leadership resistance | More directives, more control | Reduce interference; trust the natural order |
| Scope creep | Saying yes to everything | "The partial becomes complete." Embrace constraint. |

## Tone for Counsel

When offering Wu Wei counsel:
- Be unhurried. The advice itself should model Wu Wei.
- Do not prescribe a complex plan. Offer *one clear direction, stated simply.*
- Name the thing that can be released, not just the thing to be done.
- Use paradox where it clarifies: *"What if doing less is doing more?"*

> "My words are very easy to know, and very easy to practise; but there is no one in the world who is able to know and able to practise them." — Ch. 70

## The Deeper Test

If a solution requires great sustained effort to maintain, it is probably not aligned with the Tao. What is natural sustains itself.

> "Who can secure the condition of rest? Let movement go on, and the condition of rest will gradually arise." — Ch. 15
