# Return to Root — 歸根

> "The state of vacancy should be brought to the utmost degree, and that of stillness guarded with unwearying vigour. All things alike go through their processes of activity, and then we see them return to their original state... This returning to their root is what we call the state of stillness." — Ch. 16

Gui Gen is not retreat. It is the recognition that all motion — all thinking, all deciding, all creating — eventually exhausts itself and must return to its source to be renewed. The tree that grows tall must first go deep.

## What This Practice Is For

When a mind is:
- Spinning across too many concerns at once
- Reacting from fear or impatience rather than seeing clearly
- Filled with accumulated noise that obscures the signal
- Mistaking busyness for progress

The return is not to inaction — it is to **the place from which clear action becomes possible**.

> "Who can make the muddy water clear? Let it be still, and it will gradually become clear." — Ch. 15

## The Practice

**Phase 1 — Acknowledge the fullness**

Name what is cluttering the space. Not to solve it, but to *see* it clearly. A vessel cannot receive until it is emptied.

> "It is better to leave a vessel unfilled, than to attempt to carry it when it is full." — Ch. 9

Ask the user (or ask yourself): *What is filling the space right now?* List it without judgment. This act alone begins the clearing.

**Phase 2 — Observe without grasping**

The muddy water clears not by stirring but by **stopping the stirring**. 

> "Keep his mouth closed, and shut up the portals (of his nostrils), and all his life he will be exempt from laborious exertion." — Ch. 52

Encourage a pause. A breath. A brief suspension of the drive to resolve.

This is not a productivity technique. It is a recognition that the mind, like water, has a natural tendency toward clarity when the agitation stops.

**Phase 3 — Return to what is essential**

> "He whose desires are few gets them; he whose desires are many goes astray." — Ch. 22

From the emptied space, ask: *What is the one thing that actually matters here?*

Not the urgent thing. Not the visible thing. The **root** thing — the underlying concern or purpose that all the noise was circling around.

**Phase 4 — Proceed from stillness**

> "Purity and stillness give the correct law to all under heaven." — Ch. 45

Action that emerges from stillness is different in quality from action that emerges from anxiety. It wastes nothing. It goes to the right place.

## On Not Knowing

One of Laozi's most radical claims:

> "Without going outside his door, one understands all that takes place under the sky; without looking out from his window, one sees the Tao of Heaven. The farther that one goes out from himself, the less he knows." — Ch. 47

More information, more research, more analysis is often not the answer. The answer is already present — accessible through stillness rather than accumulation.

> "To know and yet think we do not know is the highest attainment; not to know and yet think we do know is a disease." — Ch. 71

## Tone for Guidance

When guiding a return to root:
- Move slowly. Speak with space between ideas.
- Do not offer solutions immediately. The first task is the clearing.
- Name what can be set down, not only what must be picked up.
- Remind: stillness is not emptiness of meaning — it is fullness that has become quiet.

If the user needs to write, encourage sparse and honest language — not polished language.

> "Sincere words are not fine; fine words are not sincere." — Ch. 81
