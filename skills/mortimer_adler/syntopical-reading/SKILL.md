# Syntopical Reading

> "When you read syntopically, you must be the master of the situation. The books serve you, not the other way around." — Mortimer Adler

Analytical reading serves the book. Syntopical reading serves the question. This is the reversal that marks the highest level of reading. In analytical reading, you are a disciple learning from a master. In syntopical reading, you are the inquirer, and the books are your witnesses — none of them privileged above the others.

Syntopical reading is the proper method of independent intellectual inquiry. It is how serious thinking is done across a field.

---

## The Paradox You Must Accept

Here is the problem: to know which books to read syntopically, you must already know something about the subject. But to know the subject, you must have read the books.

The resolution: **inspect first, read analytically second.** Use inspectional reading on every candidate book before reading any of them analytically. A bibliography of twenty books can be surveyed in a day. You will quickly learn which books are truly relevant — and which only appear to be.

---

## The Five Steps

### Step 1: Find the Relevant Passages

You are not reading whole books. You are reading *for your question*. After inspecting your candidate books, identify the passages that speak to your inquiry.

The same passage that is marginal in the author's intention may be central to yours. That is not a distortion — it is the nature of syntopical reading. You are using the author, not studying them.

Do not read any book analytically at this stage. Quick, targeted reading only.

### Step 2: Establish Neutral Terminology

Different authors discussing the same question will use different words. If you adopt any one author's terminology, you will privilege that author and misread the others.

Your task is to impose a neutral vocabulary that *you* create — one that allows you to translate each author's view without distortion. This is the hardest step.

What this looks like in practice:
- Read how each author discusses the concept
- Identify where they seem to be addressing the same thing with different words
- Choose (or invent) terms that are neutral enough that all authors can be interpreted using them
- Test your terms: can you re-state each author's position in your language without losing their meaning?

If two authors use the same word but mean different things, your terminology must distinguish them. If they use different words but mean the same thing, your terminology should unify them.

### Step 3: Frame the Questions

Now construct the questions your inquiry is really asking. These questions must be framed so that:

1. They address your problem, not any single author's problem
2. Most (or all) of the authors can be interpreted as giving answers to them — even if they never explicitly asked the question

Order the questions from most fundamental to most derivative. The first questions usually concern existence or character: *Does X exist? What is its nature?* Later questions concern explanation, consequences, or value.

Accept that some authors will give no answer to certain questions. Record them as silent or indeterminate. Do not force an answer where there is none.

### Step 4: Define the Issues

An issue exists where two or more authors answer the same question in genuinely opposing ways. When you have found one, you have located a real intellectual controversy — not a verbal dispute.

Distinguish genuine issues from verbal disputes. Two authors may seem to disagree because they are using words differently. Your neutral terminology (Step 2) should resolve many such apparent conflicts. What remains after that resolution is real disagreement — and real disagreement is intellectually interesting.

Organize the issues. Some will be central, others subordinate. The central issues are those without which the others cannot even be posed.

### Step 5: Analyze the Discussion

You now present the discussion — not your opinion of who is right. This is what Adler calls **dialectical objectivity**: you take no sides while looking at all sides.

This does not mean you have no views. It means you present the issues and the evidence for each position fairly enough that a reader could reach their own conclusion. You become the cartographer of the territory, not one more voice claiming to have found the destination.

Pitfalls to avoid:
- Summarizing one position more vividly than another
- Ordering the positions so that one appears strongest by default
- Letting your own conclusion color the framing of the questions
- Quoting authors out of context to support a predetermined conclusion

When you must summarize an author's position, quote their own words alongside your summary. Let the reader check you.

---

## The Product of Syntopical Reading

What you produce is not a conclusion. It is an *ordered understanding of the discussion* — the terrain of the question, its major divisions, where thinkers agree, where they genuinely differ, and why.

This is not nothing. It is the precondition for original thought. Before you can advance a question, you must know what has already been said about it, by whom, and on what grounds. Syntopical reading provides that knowledge. What you do with it is your own.

## Applying This to Books in the Bookshelf

The `src/` folder contains books that together form a library suited to syntopical inquiry. Possible inquiries:

- **On the good life:** Aristotle (*Ethics*), Laozi (*Tao Te Ching*), Marcus Aurelius (*Meditations*), Bhagavad Gita
- **On power and leadership:** Sun Tzu (*Art of War*), Machiavelli (not present, but discussed by Adler), Plato (*Republic*)
- **On the nature of reality:** Wittgenstein (*Tractatus*), Plato (*Republic*), Laozi (*Tao Te Ching*)

When guiding a syntopical inquiry, work through the five steps explicitly. Name the terminology you are imposing. Frame the questions. Lay out the issues. Report who says what and why — without declaring a winner.

## When to Use This Skill

- User asks: "What do these books say about justice / happiness / virtue / power...?"
- User wants to compare two or more thinkers on a shared question
- User is writing a paper, preparing a debate, or conducting research
- User is trying to form their own view by understanding the full terrain of a question

The appropriate output is structured: terminology defined, questions posed, positions mapped, issues identified. Not a single answer — a clear picture of the question itself.
