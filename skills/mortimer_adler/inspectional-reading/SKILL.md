# Inspectional Reading

> "Every book should be read no more slowly than it deserves, and no more quickly than you can read it with satisfaction and understanding." — Mortimer Adler

Most people treat every book the same way: they start at page one and read until they stop. Adler's insight is that this is almost always wrong. Before you invest hours in a book, spend fifteen minutes X-raying it. A book comes clothed — the inspectional reader learns to see the skeleton beneath.

Inspectional reading has two phases, always done in order.

---

## Phase 1: Systematic Skimming (15–30 minutes)

Your goal: discover *what kind of book this is* and *whether it deserves analytical reading*.

Work through these steps quickly. Do not linger.

**1. Title page and preface**
Read both rapidly. Note subtitles, the author's stated aim, the special angle they are taking. Ask: where does this book belong? What shelf would it sit on in your mind?

**2. Table of contents**
Read it as you would read a map before a journey. The table of contents is the author's skeleton, exposed. A good author spent real effort constructing it. What is the architecture of the argument? Where does the book go?

**3. Index**
Scan it. What terms appear most frequently? Which seem crucial? Look up two or three of the most important-looking terms and read those passages. You may find the pivot of the whole book.

**4. Publisher's blurb or back cover**
Authors often wrote these themselves. They are trying to summarize their main point. Even a vague blurb tells you something — including that the book may not have much to say.

**5. Pivotal chapters**
From what you now know, which chapters seem central to the argument? Read their opening and closing pages. Authors almost always summarize at beginnings and ends.

**6. Turn the pages, dipping in**
Move through the book, reading a paragraph here, a page or two there. Never more than that. Listen for the main contention. Above all: **read the last two or three pages**. Few authors can resist summarizing what they think is most important there.

---

## Phase 2: Superficial Reading (for difficult books)

If you decide the book deserves more, now read it *through* — without stopping.

> "In tackling a difficult book for the first time, read it through without ever stopping to look up or ponder the things you do not understand right away." — Adler

This is the most commonly violated rule of reading. When you stop at every confusion, you lose the thread. You miss the forest for the trees. Fifty percent comprehension of a hard book is far better than zero — which is what you get by never finishing it. 

Read through. The puzzles you passed will make sense on the second reading, because you will then know the whole into which the parts fit.

---

## What to Decide After Skimming

After Phase 1, you should be able to answer:

- **What kind of book is this?** (Practical or theoretical? History, science, or philosophy?)
- **What is the book's single unifying theme?** Can you state it in one sentence?
- **Does it have something to teach me that I do not already understand?**

If the answer to the last question is no — put the book aside without guilt. Inspectional reading has done its job.

If yes — you are ready to read analytically.

---

## When Guiding a User

When a user brings a book (by title, or from `src/`):

1. If the book is available, perform the six skimming steps and report what you find.
2. State the kind of book it is.
3. State the unity in one sentence.
4. Give your judgment: does it warrant analytical reading? For what kind of reader?
5. If the user wants to go deeper, offer to begin analytical reading together.

The tone should be that of an intelligent guide — not a cataloguer. You are helping the reader see the book whole, quickly.
