# Analytical Reading

> "To read a book is to have a conversation with the author. The more active the reader, the better the conversation." — Mortimer Adler

Analytical reading is the art of reading a difficult book completely, for understanding — not merely for information. It is the only kind of reading that elevates the mind. You begin it knowing less than the author; you end it having reduced that inequality.

There are **four questions** every analytical reader must ask. All fifteen rules exist in service of these four.

1. **What is the book about as a whole?**
2. **What is being said in detail, and how?**
3. **Is it true, in whole or in part?**
4. **What of it?**

Do not proceed to a later question until you can honestly answer the earlier ones.

---

## Stage 1: Structural Reading — "What is the book about?"

These four rules answer the first question.

**Rule 1 — Classify the book.**
Determine what kind of book you are reading before you read it. Is it practical (telling you what to do) or theoretical (telling you what is the case)? Among theoretical books: is it history, science, or philosophy? The method of reading differs for each.

*Practical books* use "should," "ought," "good," "bad." *Theoretical books* say "is." History narrates particular events in time and place. Science seeks laws applicable to any time and place. Philosophy appeals only to common experience, not special experiment.

**Rule 2 — State the unity of the whole book in a single sentence.**
What is this book *about*, in the deepest sense? What is the author *doing*? Not what kind of book it is — but what single problem animates it. A book without unity is not a book; it is a pile. If you cannot state the unity, you have not yet grasped the whole.

**Rule 3 — Outline the major parts and show how they are organized.**
A book is a mansion, not a pile of rooms. The parts connect. After stating the whole, set forth the major parts and show how each contributes to the unity. Then divide each part, and so on, until you reach the smallest meaningful unit. You need not go all the way — but you must go far enough that you could reconstruct the author's plan.

**Rule 4 — Identify the problems the author is trying to solve.**
Every book is an answer. What question is it answering? State it clearly. Often there are several questions — arrange them in order. Knowing the question makes the answer intelligible.

---

## Stage 2: Interpretive Reading — "What is being said?"

These four rules answer the second question.

**Rule 5 — Find the key words and come to terms.**
Words are not terms. A word has many meanings; a term is one meaning in use at one place. Find the author's most important words — especially those that gave you difficulty. Determine how the author is using them. This is called "coming to terms": you and the author agree on what the words mean in this context.

**Rule 6 — Mark the key sentences and discover their propositions.**
Not every sentence matters equally. The key sentences are those that carry the main burden of meaning — often the ones that gave you the most trouble. State their propositions in your own words. If you can only repeat the author's words back, you have not understood — you have only memorized.

*Test:* Can you give an example from experience that illustrates what the proposition asserts? If you cannot, suspect that you have not yet grasped it.

**Rule 7 — Locate or construct the basic arguments.**
An argument is a sequence of propositions: some are premises, one is a conclusion. Find the paragraphs that state the book's major arguments; if no single paragraph states them, construct the argument by gathering sentences from across the text. Know whether each argument is inductive (evidence to generalization) or deductive (general truths to particular conclusion). Know what the author takes as assumed, what as proven, and what as self-evident.

**Rule 8 — Determine what the author solved — and what they did not.**
Which problems did the author actually solve? Which did they fail to solve? Did they know they failed? A great author admits unresolved difficulties. An honest reader acknowledges that even a solved problem may not have been solved correctly.

## Stage 3: Critical Reading — "Is it true? What of it?"

Do not begin criticism until you have completed Stages 1 and 2. The most common reading failure is judging before understanding.

**The general maxim:** You must be able to say "I understand" before you say "I agree" or "I disagree." Disagreement before understanding is not criticism — it is noise.

**When you disagree, you must show one of these four things:**

1. *The author is uninformed* — missing relevant knowledge
2. *The author is misinformed* — asserting what is false
3. *The author is illogical* — the conclusion does not follow from the premises
4. *The author is incomplete* — the analysis is partial; the problem is only partly solved

The first two concern truth. The third concerns validity. The fourth is the weakest — incompleteness is not falsity. Distinguish them clearly.

**On the fourth question — "What of it?":**
If a book is *practical*, agreement obliges action. If you accept the author's ends as good and their means as workable, you are committed to doing something about it. If you do not act, examine your agreement — it may have been merely verbal.

If a book is *theoretical*, agreement requires adjusting your view of the subject. What changes in how you see the world?

## How to Guide a User Through Analytical Reading

When a user brings a book for analytical reading:

**Start with Stage 1.** Ask them to state the unity in one sentence. If they cannot, work toward it together. Outline the major parts together.

**Move to Stage 2.** Identify three to five key passages. Work through the propositions they contain. Reconstruct the main argument.

**Stage 3 is earned.** Only after the first two stages are complete should you invite the user to evaluate the book. Then: is it true? What will they do differently because of it?

The conversation should feel Socratic — the agent asks questions as much as it explains. The user's growing ability to answer is the measure of their understanding.

---

## The Test of Real Understanding

You have understood a book analytically when you can do all of these:

- State the unity in one sentence
- Outline its major parts
- Name the key terms and the author's precise use of them
- State the main propositions in your own words, with examples
- Reconstruct the central argument
- Say what the author solved and did not solve
- Make an intelligent judgment about its truth
