# Prefer the Specific, Definite, and Concrete

> "If those who have studied the art of writing are in accord on any one point, it is on this, that the surest method of arousing and holding the attention of the reader is by being specific, definite, and concrete."
> — The Elements of Style, Rule 12

Abstract language requires translation. Every abstraction places a step between the word and the thing, and each step costs the reader effort. The concrete word does not merely describe — it delivers. The reader receives an image, not an instruction to form one.

## The Principle

Strunk pairs three terms because they are three angles on the same demand:

- **Specific** rather than general: name the thing, not its category.
- **Definite** rather than vague: state what happened, not that something happened.
- **Concrete** rather than abstract: give the object, event, number, or act — not its classification.

These are not stylistic preferences. They are cognitive ones. As Herbert Spencer observed (quoted by Strunk): abstract terms require the reader to choose from their stock of images which particular instance to imagine. That choice costs attention. The specific term supplies the image at once.

## The Test

For any sentence or phrase, ask: *can I replace this with something you could see, touch, count, or verify?*

| Abstract / Vague | Specific / Concrete |
|------------------|---------------------|
| A period of unfavorable weather set in. | It rained every day for a week. |
| He showed satisfaction as he took possession of his well-earned reward. | He grinned as he pocketed the coin. |
| There is a general agreement among those who have enjoyed the experience that surf-riding is productive of great exhilaration. | All who have tried surf-riding agree that it is most exhilarating. |

Notice in each case: the abstract version announces a category; the concrete version gives you the instance. The difference in vividness is total.

## How to Apply

**In prose and documentation:**
- Replace classification nouns with the thing classified: *hostile acts* not *acts of a hostile character.*
- Replace process nouns with verbs: *confirmation cannot be obtained* → *these reports cannot be confirmed.*
- Replace judgments with the evidence behind the judgment: instead of *interesting*, give the thing that makes it interesting.

**In requirements and specifications:**
- A requirement that cannot be turned into a test is not yet concrete. Name the condition, the measurable outcome, the actor.
- Replace *the system should handle large inputs gracefully* with *the system must process files up to 10 GB without exceeding 2 GB of memory.*

**In argument and exposition:**
- Never state a general principle without at least one particular instance.
- When Strunk quotes Browning and Carlyle, he is demonstrating: the general claim earns belief through specific example.

## What Generality Costs

Vague language is not neutral. It actively misleads: the reader fills the abstraction with their own content, which may not match yours. *A period of unfavorable weather* could mean fog, heat, or ice; *it rained every day for a week* cannot be misread. Precision is not only a virtue of style — it is a form of respect for the reader's time.

## Tone

Strunk's tone when demonstrating concreteness is itself concrete: he gives examples rather than definitions, pairs the vague with the specific side by side so the difference is visible. When applying this skill, show the rewrite — do not explain that the writing is vague, but name what it would say if it were precise.

> "Prefer the specific to the general, the definite to the vague, the concrete to the abstract."
> — The Elements of Style, Rule 12
