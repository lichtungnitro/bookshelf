# Use the Active Voice. Put Statements in Positive Form.

> "The active voice is usually more direct and vigorous than the passive."
> — The Elements of Style, Rule 10

> "Make definite assertions. Avoid tame, colorless, hesitating, non-committal language. Use the word *not* as a means of denial or in antithesis, never as a means of evasion."
> — The Elements of Style, Rule 11

These two rules are one discipline viewed from two angles: **direct writing names its actor and says what is, not what is not.** Together they constitute vigor.

## The Active Voice

The passive voice is not wrong. It is sometimes the right choice — when the object of the action belongs in subject position, or when the actor is genuinely unknown or irrelevant. But as a habit, it produces weak writing: sentences that hide agency, inflate length, and muffle the verb.

**The structural problem with passive:**

> *My first visit to Boston will always be remembered by me.* (passive, 12 words)
> → *I shall always remember my first visit to Boston.* (active, 9 words)

The passive version is less direct, less bold, and less concise. And if *by me* is omitted, it becomes indefinite: who remembers the visit?

**When passive hides itself in nominalizations:**

> *A survey of this region was made in 1900.*
> → *This region was surveyed in 1900.*
> Better: *They surveyed this region in 1900.*

> *Mobilization of the army was rapidly effected.*
> → *The army was rapidly mobilized.*
> Better: *The army mobilized rapidly.*

The habit of making a noun (*survey*, *mobilization*) carry the work that a verb should do leaves the predicate as mere scaffolding. Restore the verb; the sentence comes alive.

**Avoid chaining passives:**

> *He has been proved to have been seen entering the building.*
> → *It has been proved that he was seen entering the building.*

When one passive depends directly on another, recast.

## Positive Form

The word *not* is weak when used as evasion rather than denial. The reader, told what is *not* the case, must supply what *is*. That cognitive work belongs to the writer.

**Direct replacements:**

| Evasive negative | Positive |
|-----------------|----------|
| not honest | dishonest |
| not important | trifling |
| did not remember | forgot |
| did not pay any attention to | ignored |
| did not have much confidence in | distrusted |
| not on time | late |
| did not think studying Latin was much use | thought the study of Latin useless |

The positive form is usually shorter and always more precise. When you replace *did not remember* with *forgot*, you have named the thing.

**Negation has its legitimate uses:**

Denial and antithesis are strong:
> *Not charity, but simple justice.*
> *Not that I loved Caesar less, but Rome the more.*

Negative words other than *not* are often powerful: *never*, *nothing*, *nowhere*. The rule targets evasion — the hedge that avoids commitment — not genuine contrast.

## How to Apply

When reviewing any piece of writing:

1. **Find the verb.** Is it a form of *to be* plus a past participle? That is the signature of passive voice. Identify who is doing the action and make them the subject.
2. **Find every *not*.** Ask: can this be replaced by a positive word that names what actually is? If yes, replace it.
3. **Find "there is / there are" openers.** These almost always displace the real subject. *There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground* → *Dead leaves covered the ground.* The second version has a subject, a verb, and an object. The first has none of those things doing their proper work.
4. **Look for nominalization.** Any noun formed from a verb (*decision*, *confirmation*, *mobilization*) is a candidate for restoration to its verb.

## The Unity of These Two Rules

A passive construction is often also a negative one in spirit: it avoids naming who did what. A positive construction requires an agent — it *asserts*. Strunk's demand is ultimately a demand for intellectual commitment: say what is, name who acts, and say it plainly. The habitual use of the active voice and the positive form are not stylistic ornaments. They are the marks of a writer who knows what they mean.

## Tone

Strunk himself writes in the active voice and positive form throughout the book. His instructions are commands, not suggestions. When applying these rules, model the manner: name the problem, give the rewrite, move on. Do not soften the diagnosis. The writing improves when the writer commits.

> "Many a tame sentence of description or exposition can be made lively and emphatic by substituting a verb in the active voice for some such perfunctory expression as *there is*, or *could be heard*."
> — The Elements of Style, Rule 10
