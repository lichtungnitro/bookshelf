# Omit Needless Words

> "Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts."
> — The Elements of Style, Rule 13

This does not require short sentences, bare outlines, or eliminated detail. It requires that every word tell. The difference is between *trimming fat* and *removing flesh*: cut what does not work; keep what does.

## The Discipline

The test for any word, phrase, or sentence is: *does removing it change the meaning or weaken the force?* If not, remove it.

Most wordiness falls into three families:

**1. Hollow introductions**

These phrases delay the real sentence without adding anything:

| Wordy | Direct |
|-------|--------|
| the question as to whether | whether |
| there is no doubt but that | doubtless |
| in a hasty manner | hastily |
| this is a subject which | this subject |
| owing to the fact that | since (because) |
| I was unaware of the fact that | I did not know that |
| call your attention to the fact that | remind you |

The expression *the fact that* is especially invasive. Revise it out of every sentence in which it occurs.

**2. Superfluous relatives**

*Who is*, *which was*, and similar constructions are often dead weight:

> His brother, *who is* a member of the same firm → His brother, a member of the same firm
> Trafalgar, *which was* Nelson's last battle → Trafalgar, Nelson's last battle

**3. Sprawl across sentences**

A single complex idea broken into separate statements that could be one sentence is verbose by construction:

> *Macbeth was very ambitious. This led him to wish to become king of Scotland. The witches told him that this wish of his would come true...* (51 words.)
> → *Encouraged by his wife, Macbeth achieved his ambition and realized the prediction of the witches by murdering Duncan and becoming king of Scotland in his place.* (26 words.)

The condensed version is not just shorter — it is stronger.

## How to Apply

When reviewing writing:

1. **Find the real sentence.** Ask: what is the one thing this is saying? Strip to that.
2. **Test every modifier.** Does the adjective, adverb, or clause add information the noun or verb doesn't already carry?
3. **Collapse series into compounds.** If three sentences say one thing, they should be one sentence.
4. **Kill nominalization.** *He made a decision* → *He decided.* *The surveying of the region* → *The region was surveyed.* Better: *They surveyed the region.*
5. **Cut the preamble.** The first sentence of any draft often exists only to warm up. Delete it and see if the paragraph improves.

Note that positive and active constructions (see Rules 10–11) are also more concise. Many examples of wordiness are simultaneously examples of passivity and negativity.

## Tone

Strunk is brisk, even impatient. He does not hedge the rule: "make every word tell." When applying this discipline, do not apologize for cutting. The writing is improved, not diminished. The goal is not austerity but *force*: the sentence that says the thing, and nothing else.

> "This requires not that the writer make all his sentences short, or that he avoid all detail and treat his subjects only in outline, but that he make every word tell."
> — The Elements of Style, Rule 13
