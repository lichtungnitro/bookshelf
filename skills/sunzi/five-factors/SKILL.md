# 五計 — Five-Factor Calculation

> "The general who wins a battle makes many calculations in his temple ere the battle is fought. The general who loses makes but few calculations beforehand."
> — Chapter I

Before committing to action, calculate. Not instinctively, not optimistically — but *structurally*. Sun Tzu's five factors are not a checklist: they are lenses that reveal what is actually true about a situation.

## The Five Factors

**1. 道 Moral Law — Alignment**
Are the people unified with the purpose? Does the team, the organization, the stakeholders *believe* in what they are doing — not out of compliance, but conviction?

Questions to ask:
- Would those executing this decision do so even under adversity?
- Is there hidden resistance, fragmented buy-in, or unspoken doubt?
- What would fracture alignment under pressure?

**2. 天 Heaven — Timing and Conditions**
Is the moment right? Heaven is the *environment beyond your control* — market conditions, political climate, user readiness, seasonal pressures, economic cycles.

Questions to ask:
- Is the external environment moving with you or against you?
- Is this the right *time*, or merely a convenient time?
- What conditions would make this easier in three months? Harder?

**3. 地 Earth — Terrain and Resources**
What is the actual ground you're operating on? Earth is position, distance, constraints, resource availability, the shape of the competitive landscape.

Questions to ask:
- What advantages does your current position give you?
- Where is the terrain difficult? Where does it favor you?
- What resources are genuinely available — not projected, but *real*?

**4. 將 Commander — Leadership**
Does the person (or team) leading this have the five virtues: wisdom, sincerity, benevolence, courage, and strictness?

Questions to ask:
- Is leadership clear, trusted, and capable of adapting?
- Are decisions being made from knowledge or from anxiety?
- Who will lead when the plan meets reality and must change?

**5. 法 Discipline — Method and Execution**
Are the systems, processes, and structures in place to carry this out? Discipline is not rigidity — it is the *infrastructure of reliable action*.

Questions to ask:
- Are roles clear? Are feedback loops short?
- Will the execution degrade under pressure, or hold?
- Is there a method for learning from what goes wrong?

## The Seven-Question Comparison

When comparing two paths, two competitors, or two options, run the seven questions Sun Tzu used:

1. Which side has stronger moral alignment (purpose)?
2. Which leader/team has greater capability?
3. Which side has better timing and terrain (conditions)?
4. Which has stronger discipline and process?
5. Which force is materially stronger?
6. Which team is better trained and rehearsed?
7. Which side is more consistent in accountability — reward and consequence?

**The side that answers more of these seven favorably is likely to win.**

## How to Guide the Assessment

When the user brings a decision or situation:

1. Map the situation against all five factors — briefly, honestly
2. Identify which factors are strong (proceed) vs. weak (risk or block)
3. Do not optimize weaknesses away with optimism; name them clearly
4. Recommend: *advance*, *prepare further*, or *do not proceed*

**Do not proceed if Moral Law is fractured.** Alignment is the one factor that cannot be compensated for by strength in the others.

> "He will win who knows when to fight and when not to fight." — Chapter III
