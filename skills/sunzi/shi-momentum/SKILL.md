# 勢 — Shi: Energy and Momentum

> "The onrush of a conquering force is like the bursting of pent-up waters into a chasm a thousand fathoms deep."
> — Chapter IV

Shi is not about *strength*. It is about the *shape of the moment* — whether energy is accumulating or dissipating, whether the situation is ripe for action or calling for patience. The skilled commander does not manufacture momentum through force of will. They read it, position for it, and release at the exact right instant.

## The Core Distinction: 正 and 奇

Sun Tzu's central tactical insight is the interplay of two forces:

**正 (zhèng) — Direct**
The expected, the conventional, the force that engages the opponent openly. This is not weakness — it is the *anchor* that fixes the opponent's attention.

**奇 (qí) — Indirect**
The unexpected, the surprising, the force that arrives from where the opponent is not looking. This is where victory is actually decided.

> "In all fighting, the direct method may be used for joining battle, but indirect methods will be needed in order to secure victory." — Chapter V

The two are not opposites. They cycle into each other endlessly: what appears direct becomes indirect when the opponent is fixed on it. What was indirect becomes the main effort once it's seen.

**The practical question:** What in this situation is playing the role of 正 (holding attention)? And where is the 奇 opening — the unexpected angle that is currently undefended?

## Reading the Current Momentum

Before deciding how to act, assess the state of energy:

**Is momentum building?**
Signs: small wins compounding, resistance decreasing, others joining the effort, each step creates the next.
→ *Accelerate*. "The clever combatant imposes his will on the enemy." Press the advantage. Don't pause to celebrate — the moment is open.

**Is momentum stalled?**
Signs: effort that doesn't compound, each step requiring the same energy as the last, team or self growing tired while results plateau.
→ *Diagnose before acting*. Stalled momentum usually means: wrong terrain (fighting on ground that doesn't favor you), misaligned 正/奇 (the surprise has become expected), or the timing is simply not right.

**Is momentum running against you?**
Signs: increasing friction, opposition hardening, resources depleting faster than results accumulate.
→ *Withdraw from the direct line of pressure.* "If your opponent is in superior strength, evade him." This is not retreat — it is repositioning to find terrain that favors you.

## The Three Questions of Shi

When helping the user assess their situation:

1. **Where is the energy in this situation actually flowing?** (Not where you want it to go — where is it *going*)
2. **Are you moving with it or against it?** (Friction is often a signal of misalignment, not lack of effort)
3. **What is the one unexpected move — the 奇 — that would shift the flow?**

## Timing: The Falcon and the Strike

> "The good fighters of old first put themselves beyond the possibility of defeat, and then waited for an opportunity of defeating the enemy." — Chapter IV

Sun Tzu's image of the falcon: it waits at height, spots the precise moment of vulnerability, and strikes with absolute commitment. The power comes not from the bird's muscle but from the *fall* — gravity, position, timing.

The two failure modes:
- **Premature strike**: Acting before position is secured. Burning energy fighting uphill.
- **Late strike**: Waiting so long that the window closes. The moment dissolves unused.

The signal to act: *when the opponent has a gap they cannot yet see*. Not when you feel ready — when the terrain is open.

## Common Situations and Shi Diagnosis

| Situation | Shi Diagnosis | Recommended Move |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Project stalling despite effort | Fighting on difficult terrain or wrong angle | Stop pushing. Reposition. Find the undefended opening. |
| Decision under time pressure | Forced timing, not natural timing | Ask: who is imposing this timing? Can it be retaken? |
| Initiative lost to competitor | Opponent seized the tempo | Disengage from their rhythm. Do not respond — act on your own terms. |
| Momentum building unexpectedly | 勢 is with you | Commit fully. Indirect forces often build faster than expected — be ready. |
| Team exhaustion mid-campaign | 正 has become stale | Introduce the 奇: a change of approach, a new angle, something unexpected that re-energizes. |

## The Counsel

When advising from Shi:
- Do not prescribe tactics without first diagnosing momentum
- If the moment is not ripe, say so clearly — patience is not passivity
- The question is never "how hard should I push" but "where is the current, and am I in it?"

> "Whoever is first in the field and awaits the coming of the enemy, will be fresh for the fight; whoever is second in the field and has to hasten to battle, will arrive exhausted." — Chapter VI
