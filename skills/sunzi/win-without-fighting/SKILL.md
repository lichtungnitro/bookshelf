# 不戰而屈人之兵 — Win Without Fighting

> "Supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting."
> — Chapter III

This is Sun Tzu's most profound principle. Not the strongest insight about *how* to fight — but the insight that the best strategists rarely need to fight at all. Victory through stratagem is higher than victory through battle.

## The Hierarchy of Stratagem

Sun Tzu gives four tiers, ranked from highest to lowest:

```
1. 伐謀 — Attack the Plan          (highest)
2. 伐交 — Attack the Alliances
3. 伐兵 — Attack the Army in the Field
4. 攻城 — Besiege the Fortress     (lowest, last resort)
```

**Work from the top down.** Direct confrontation is not failure — it is simply the most expensive and least elegant form of winning. Exhaust higher tiers before descending.

## Applying the Hierarchy

**Tier 1: Attack the Plan**
Before the opponent moves, undermine the logic that would make their action succeed. Reshape the situation so their strategy becomes irrelevant.

Modern equivalents:
- In product strategy: build the feature that makes their roadmap obsolete
- In negotiation: reframe the terms so your offer becomes the obvious choice
- In organizational change: build such clear consensus that opposition has no room to coalesce
- In technical decisions: demonstrate a working proof so the debate ends without argument

**Tier 2: Attack the Alliances**
Separate the opponent from their support. Without coalition, even strong opposition collapses.

Modern equivalents:
- Win over the skeptic who is giving the blocker their credibility
- Build relationships with the people whose endorsement your opponent depends on
- Isolate the argument by addressing the underlying fears of those who would otherwise amplify it

**Tier 3: Attack in the Field**
When the first two tiers are exhausted or unavailable — engage directly, but with preparation, timing, and position already in your favor. Never enter the field without having first tilted it.

> "He will win who, prepared himself, waits to take the enemy unprepared." — Chapter III

**Tier 4: Besiege the Fortress**
Last resort. Expensive, slow, demoralizing. Avoid if possible.

> "The general, unable to control his irritation, will launch his men to the assault like swarming ants, with the result that one-third of his men are slain, while the town still remains untaken." — Chapter III

When the user is already at this tier: do not judge. Help them siege efficiently — but also check whether a negotiated exit or repositioning is still possible.

## The Three Questions Before Acting

Before any direct confrontation, ask:

1. **What does the opponent actually want?** (Their goal, not their position)
2. **What do *I* actually want?** (My goal, not my stated demand)
3. **Is there a path where both goals are satisfied — without the fight?**

If the goals are truly incompatible: proceed to the hierarchy above. If they are compatible: the fight was never necessary.

## What "Intact" Means

> "To take the enemy's country whole and intact — this is the highest art. To shatter and destroy it is not so good."

Victory that preserves the opponent's capacity is superior to victory that destroys it. A subdued competitor can become an ally. A destroyed one leaves only rubble and resentment — and sets a precedent that you will fight to the death, which hardens every future opponent.

In modern terms: win in a way that the other side can still function. The most durable victories are ones where the losing side does not feel humiliated.

## Tone for Counsel

When guiding a user through this framework:
- Do not begin with tactics. Begin by questioning whether the fight is necessary.
- Name the tier they are currently at and ask: have the higher tiers been genuinely tried?
- If they choose to fight, help them fight from a position already prepared — not reactively.
- Remind them: elegance is not weakness. The strategist who wins without fighting wins *more*.

> "Thus the highest form of generalship is to balk the enemy's plans; the next best is to prevent the junction of the enemy's forces; the next in order is to attack the enemy's army in the field; and the worst policy of all is to besiege walled cities." — Chapter III
