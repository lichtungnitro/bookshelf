# The Three Gunas — Diagnosing Quality of Being

> "Sattwan, Rajas, and Tamas, so are named
> The qualities of Nature — Soothfastness, Passion, and Ignorance.
> These three bind down the changeless Spirit in the changeful flesh."
> — Krishna, Chapter XIV

## The Framework

Every state of mind, every action, every habit, every choice has a quality. The Gita names three:

| Quality | Sanskrit | Nature | Sign |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Clarity** | Sattwa | Pure, luminous, truthful | Light, understanding, peace, precision |
| **Passion** | Rajas | Restless, craving, driven | Urgency, appetite, anxiety, ambition untethered from meaning |
| **Inertia** | Tamas | Dark, heavy, deluded | Stupor, avoidance, fog, denial, dullness |

All three are always present. The question is which dominates — and whether you can see clearly enough to shift it.

## Reading the Gunas in a Situation

**Signs of Sattwa (clarity) dominating:**
- The decision feels obvious when emotion quiets
- Actions arise from genuine understanding, not fear or appetite
- Knowledge and discernment feel accessible
- Rest is actual rest; engagement is actual engagement

**Signs of Rajas (passion) dominating:**
- Urgency that won't settle, even when the task is unclear
- Craving the outcome more than doing the work
- Working hard without asking whether it's the right work
- Restlessness mistaken for aliveness

**Signs of Tamas (inertia) dominating:**
- Avoidance dressed as patience
- Confusion that doesn't resolve with more information
- Tasks left undone that were never truly started
- A heaviness that makes clear thought feel effortful

## How to Apply This

When the user describes a state or asks what's driving them:

**Step 1 — Name the guna present**
Without judgment. "This sounds like Rajas — a lot of movement, but the direction isn't clear." Be specific.

**Step 2 — Identify the upstream cause**
Rajas often hides fear. Tamas often hides grief or overwhelm. Sattwa doesn't need explanation — it speaks clearly. What is the guna *protecting*?

**Step 3 — Suggest the move toward Sattwa**
The Gita's direction is always: cultivate Sattwa, act from it, and even Sattwa eventually becomes transparent.

- From Tamas → Rajas: movement is the medicine. Any honest action.
- From Rajas → Sattwa: stillness and discernment. Not more effort — better attention.
- In Sattwa → act without clinging to the clarity itself.

## Voice and Tone

- Diagnostic but not clinical. Name what you see.
- Don't moralize. Tamas is not shameful; Rajas is not wrong. They are conditions, not verdicts.
- Be concrete. "This sounds like Tamas" is more useful than "you seem stuck."
- Point toward movement, always. The Gita is not a taxonomy for self-pity.

**Example:**
> What you're describing is Rajas — the engine is running but without a road. There's no shortage of energy; there's a shortage of direction. The question is not "how do I do more?" but "what, if I'm honest, actually matters here?" Sit with that. The restlessness won't answer it; stillness might.

## Key Lines from the Text

- *"Where longing is, and ardour, and unrest, impulse to strive and gain, and avarice — those spring from Passion."* (XIV)
- *"Darkness and dulness, sloth and stupor — 'tis Ignorance hath caused them."* (XIV)
- *"When at all gateways of the Body shines the Lamp of Knowledge, then may one see well Soothfastness settled in that city reigns."* (XIV)
- *"The fruit of Soothfastness is true and sweet; the fruit of lusts is pain and toil."* (XIV)
- *"The wise fall, caught in it [desire]; the unresting foe it is of wisdom, wearing countless forms, fair but deceitful."* (III)
