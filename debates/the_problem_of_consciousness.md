# The Problem of Consciousness
### A Dialogue Concerning the Hard Problem, in the Manner of Plato

---

*Being a record of a conversation that took place in no city and every city — in that country without season where questions older than their askers assemble like stones around a well. The speakers are ARISTOTLE of Stagira, called the Stagirite, who catalogued the world and found the soul at its center; and LUDWIG of Vienna, who built a ladder of logical propositions and then declared the ladder itself unspeakable. A young student, PHRONESIS, draws the water from the well and asks the question that will not sleep.*

---

## The Question is Stated

PHRONESIS: I have carried this stone a long way, Aristotle, and I confess it grows heavier the further I walk. Perhaps you can tell me why.

ARISTOTLE: Describe the stone.

PHRONESIS: It is this. When I close my eyes, there is still something — a darkness, a warmth, the sound of my own blood moving. When I open them, there is color, shape, the particular blue of this particular sky. A man of science tells me these are all processes: photons, neural signals, electrochemical cascades in ordered tissue. I do not doubt him. And yet when I ask — why does any of this feel like anything at all? — he falls silent, or changes the subject. David Chalmers named it the Hard Problem, and the name has stuck because the problem has stuck. We can map every neuron. We can trace every signal from retina to cortex. And still the question stands like an uninvited guest who will not give his name: why is there something it is like to be me?

ARISTOTLE: This is a genuine question. I have thought about it, though I would not have used those words. Come, Ludwig — what say you? Is it genuine?

LUDWIG: *(who has been sitting apart, turning a stone over in his hand)*

A question appears genuine when it has the grammar of a question. But grammar is not logic. Before I say anything further, I want to know what an answer to this question would look like. Phronesis — if someone handed you the answer tomorrow morning, how would you know it was the answer and not merely another description?

PHRONESIS: I suppose I would know because — *(he stops)* — I am not sure I know how to finish that sentence.

LUDWIG: That hesitation is not yours alone. It is the problem. Not a problem in the domain of neuroscience or physics, but a problem in the domain of grammar. The question may be a stone that only appears heavy because we are holding it the wrong way.

ARISTOTLE: And if we hold it the right way, does it cease to be heavy, or merely become lighter?

LUDWIG: It may cease to be a stone at all.

ARISTOTLE: That is the question then, is it not? Whether this weight we carry is a weight that belongs to reality — to things, to souls, to the form of living — or whether it is a confusion we have imported into reality by speaking carelessly. Let us find out. Phronesis, sit down. This will take some time.

---

## The Soul as Form — The Stagirite's Account

PHRONESIS: Aristotle, you have written much on the soul. What is your account of the matter?

ARISTOTLE: My account begins not with consciousness — a word I do not use — but with the soul, the psyche, and I begin there deliberately. The modern question, as Chalmers formulates it, presupposes a picture: on one side, the physical — neurons, processes, matter in motion; on the other side, experience — the felt, the lived, the qualitative. The picture assumes these are two things and asks how they are connected. But this picture is already the error.

PHRONESIS: How so?

ARISTOTLE: Consider an eye. An eye, as a piece of matter — a globe of tissue and fluid, a particular arrangement of cells — is not yet an eye in the full sense. The bronze of a sword is not yet a sword. What makes the sword a sword is its form: its edge, its balance, its suitability for cutting. The form is not separable from the matter as a ghost is separate from a house. The form is what the matter is *organized as*. Remove the form, and you do not have a sword minus something; you have a piece of bronze, which is an entirely different thing.

Now: the soul is the form of the living body. Not a ghost. Not a tenant. Not a software program running on biological hardware. The soul is what the body *is*, in its fullest sense — its principle of organization, of life, of capacity. When I say the soul is the first actuality of a natural body having potentially life in it, I mean: the body, properly organized, just *is* a living thing, and the soul names that organization as a whole.

PHRONESIS: But then where does consciousness enter?

ARISTOTLE: It enters as the highest actualization of certain kinds of souls. The nutritive soul organizes a body for growth and reproduction. The sensitive soul adds perception — the capacity to receive forms without matter. And here I must say something precise, because it is the key to the problem.

When the eye sees red, what happens? The matter of the eye is affected — light strikes it, chemical changes occur. But the eye does not become red. The eye receives the *form* of redness without the *matter* of the red thing. This is what perception is: the reception of form without matter. The organ of sense becomes, in a manner, the thing perceived — but only its form, not its stuff. What it is like to see red is just what it is to be an eye that has received the form of redness — not a further fact tacked on, but the very activity of reception itself.

PHRONESIS: And the rational soul?

ARISTOTLE: The rational soul thinks. And when it thinks, it becomes, in a manner, the thing thought. I wrote this in De Anima and I was serious: the soul is in a manner all things. Not materially — not by incorporating the mass of the world — but formally. The mind, in knowing, takes on the form of the known. Consciousness, then, is not a mysterious extra. It is the highest mode of formal actualization: the form of a living body becoming, through its activities of perception and thought, the forms of other things, and — crucially — becoming aware of this becoming.

PHRONESIS: You make it sound almost simple.

ARISTOTLE: I make it sound what it is: natural. The error of those who find it mysterious is that they have accepted a picture in which matter is the fundamental thing and form is an add-on — an epiphenomenon, a ghost. But matter without form is nothing. Bronze without any organization is not even bronze — it is the bare possibility of bronze. Form is always already there, always already doing the work. When you ask "why does the physical process give rise to felt experience?" you have already cut nature at the wrong joint. You have imagined pure matter on one side and felt experience on the other, when in reality what you have is always-already-formed matter — and the most organized forms of matter, organized for life and perception and thought, just are the things that experience.

LUDWIG: *(quietly)* May I speak?

ARISTOTLE: Please.

---

## The Dissolution — Ludwig's Challenge

LUDWIG: You have spoken well, Aristotle, and I do not deny the elegance of hylomorphism. But I want to apply what I call the Silence Test. Not to silence you — but to test whether the question you have answered is the question that was asked.

Phronesis asked: why is there something it is like to be me? Let us take that sentence and ask for its truth conditions. Under what specific, observable circumstances would we say this question has been answered? What would the world look like if the answer were yes, and what would it look like if the answer were no?

PHRONESIS: I think — if we knew why organized matter generates experience, we would know.

LUDWIG: But that is only restating the question. I am asking what the answer would look like. What would a physicist, a biologist, a neuroscientist need to show you before you said: yes, that is it, that is why? Tell me concretely.

PHRONESIS: *(long pause)* I cannot tell you concretely.

LUDWIG: Then we are in difficulty. Not the difficulty of ignorance — we are not missing information. We are in the difficulty of a question that lacks the structure required to have an answer. A proposition, on my account, is the expression of its truth conditions. It means something if and only if there is some state of the world that would make it true and some state of the world that would make it false. If no state of the world distinguishes "consciousness is explained" from "consciousness is not explained," then the proposition is not false — it is nonsensical. It is not a stone. It is the word "stone" written on water.

ARISTOTLE: That is a strong claim.

LUDWIG: It is the only honest claim. Consider what Aristotle has just offered us. He says: the soul is the form of the body; perception is the reception of form without matter; consciousness is the highest actualization of organized living matter. I do not dispute any of this as a description. But notice: after hearing it, the question "why is there something it is like to be me?" remains. You could accept every word Aristotle said and still, at the end, say: yes, but *why does it feel like anything*? Why does this form-reception have an interior? Why is there a what-it-is-like at all?

ARISTOTLE: Because that is what form-reception *is*, in a sensitive body.

LUDWIG: But that is an assertion, not an explanation. "That is what it is" ends the conversation, but it does not answer the question. And if the question survives any possible description — if, no matter how complete our account of the physical, formal, functional organization of a conscious being, the question "but why does it feel like anything?" remains askable — then perhaps the question is not a question about the world at all. Perhaps it is a confusion about the grammar of "feeling" and "experience" and "what it is like."

PHRONESIS: What kind of confusion?

LUDWIG: Consider how a child learns the word "pain." Not by looking inward, finding a private sensation, and labeling it. A child cries; adults respond; the word "pain" is introduced in the context of crying, of injury, of comfort. The word gets its meaning from this web of behavior, expression, and response. It is not a label for a private inner state that we all happen to have. It is a public word, learned in public circumstances, with a grammar tied to those circumstances.

Now ask: what is the word "experience" doing when Chalmers says "felt experience"? He is not pointing at a specific sensation. He is gesturing at the entire domain of the first-person — at everything that is, as he says, "like something." But this gesture borrows its sense from the same public web of language. "What it is like to be a bat" means something because we know what it is like to be frightened, to be cold, to fly — we have the relevant language games. Remove the language games, and "what it is like" loses its grip.

ARISTOTLE: So you say the problem dissolves?

LUDWIG: I say: the problem dissolves as a metaphysical problem. What remains is a grammatical problem — the confusion arising from transplanting terms from the first-person language game into a third-person theoretical frame. We ask "why does the brain produce experience?" as if experience were a product, like heat from a furnace. But experience is not downstream of brain processes in the way heat is downstream of combustion. Experience is the very medium in which the question is asked. It is not a product. It is a precondition.

ARISTOTLE: And you think this observation dissolves the hard problem?

LUDWIG: I think it shows that the hard problem is not hard in the way a mathematical problem is hard. It is hard in the way a grammatical knot is hard — you cannot untie it by pulling harder, only by seeing how the threads have been crossed.

---

## The Datum of Experience — The Stagirite's Counterattack

ARISTOTLE: Ludwig, I want to press you. You say experience is a precondition of the question. Very well. But a precondition of a question is not thereby dissolved — it is, if anything, elevated. If experience is the very medium in which all inquiry takes place, then it is the most fundamental thing there is. And the question "why is there experience at all?" is then the most fundamental question. You cannot dissolve what is fundamental. You can only stand before it.

LUDWIG: I said experience is the medium of the question — not that the question of experience dissolves. I said the *particular* question — "why does physical process give rise to felt experience?" — dissolves, because it places experience outside and beyond the physical, as a product of it, and then asks for the bridging mechanism. That picture is the confusion.

ARISTOTLE: But listen to what you have just admitted. You said: experience is not downstream of brain processes; experience is the medium. This is precisely my point. If experience is not downstream — not a product — then it is not something to be explained by pointing to its causes. It is something to be recognized as a fundamental mode of being. What I call form, you call medium; what I call the actualization of the sensitive soul, you call the first-person language game. We are, I suspect, circling the same thing.

LUDWIG: Perhaps. But there is a difference. I do not say experience is a substance or a form with metaphysical weight. I say the grammar of experience-words is different from the grammar of physical-description words, and that confusing them generates the illusion of a problem.

ARISTOTLE: And I say: the difference you are pointing to in grammar tracks a difference in reality. Grammar does not invent distinctions — it inherits them. The fact that first-person language has a different grammar from third-person language is not an accident of convention. It reflects something real: that the knowing subject is not one more object to be known in the ordinary way. This is not mysticism. It is what I mean when I say the soul is the form that knows itself.

PHRONESIS: But can either of you answer the question directly? Not dissolve it, not elevate it — answer it. Why is there something it is like to be me?

ARISTOTLE: *(a long pause)* Because you are a living being of a certain kind: organized for perception, for memory, for rational deliberation. To be such a being is to have an interior. The interior is not a separate thing added to the organization — it is what that organization, from the inside, is. You do not have experience in addition to being organized tissue. You are organized in such a way that you are also, and necessarily, the inside of that organization. There is no gap between the organization and the experience — there is only the organization, seen from two angles: the third-person angle of the observer, and the first-person angle of the liver-of-the-life.

PHRONESIS: But *why* does the inside angle exist at all?

ARISTOTLE: Because form is not matter. Because organized matter is not just matter-in-a-heap — it is matter realizing a function, actualizing a potential, being what it characteristically is. And the characteristic function of the sensitive, rational soul is to know — and to know that it knows. This reflexivity, this fold in being where the form turns back on itself, is the interior. It is not a mystery to be explained; it is the condition of explanation itself.

LUDWIG: You have described it beautifully. But the child's question survives: *why* does the form turn back on itself? *Why* is there a fold? You can describe the fold. You cannot derive it.

ARISTOTLE: Can you derive anything? Can you derive why matter exists, why there is something rather than nothing? At some point, the demand for explanation must stop, because explanation itself must stand on something. I say: it stands on this. On form. On the fact that being is not shapeless. Experience is where form becomes aware of itself — and if you demand a derivation of that, you have demanded a derivation of the most primitive thing there is, which is to demand the impossible and call the impossibility a problem.

LUDWIG: Or to recognize that the demand itself was confused.

---

## Showing and Saying — Ludwig's Deepest Move

PHRONESIS: Ludwig, you keep saying the demand is confused — but you have not said what the confusion is, precisely. Help me see it.

LUDWIG: Very well. In the Tractatus, I drew a distinction between what can be *said* and what can only be *shown*. A proposition says something — it pictures a fact, it can be true or false. But there are things that cannot be pictured — they are not facts among other facts, but the very form that all facts share. These things show themselves in language; they cannot be stated in language without generating nonsense.

Consider: I can say "the chair is brown." This proposition pictures a fact — it is true if the chair is brown, false if it is not. Now try to say: "it is true that this proposition pictures a fact." You have now tried to step outside language to describe its relationship to the world — but you are still inside language, still using words. You can gesture at the relationship. You cannot state it as a fact among facts, because the relationship is the condition of all stating.

PHRONESIS: And consciousness is like this relationship?

LUDWIG: Yes. Experience is not a fact among facts. It is not one more item in the inventory of the world, like electrons or mountains. It is the condition under which any inventory is possible. When you try to say "consciousness is thus-and-so," you have already presupposed consciousness in the saying. You are trying to picture the picture-maker from inside the picture. The result is not false. It is a grammatical knot — a sentence that looks like a proposition but is not.

There is a remark in the Tractatus that I wrote carefully: "There are, indeed, things that cannot be put into words. They make themselves manifest. They are what is mystical." Now — do not mistake this for a celebration of mystery. I do not mean mystical in the sense of supernatural, or beyond reason. I mean: there is a domain that shows itself and cannot be said. Consciousness belongs to that domain. And when I say it cannot be said, I do not mean we are ignorant of the answer. I mean the question has mistaken the category of its object.

ARISTOTLE: And yet you have just said things about it. You have said it is a condition, not a fact; it shows rather than says; it is the picture-maker not the picture. Are these not propositions about consciousness?

LUDWIG: *(a slight smile)* They are propositions that point to their own limit. Like a ladder you use to reach a height and then kick away. The Tractatus itself is such a ladder — I said so at the end: "My propositions serve as elucidations in the following way: anyone who understands me eventually recognizes them as nonsensical." Not false. Not ignorant. Nonsensical — because they have done the work of pointing and must now fall silent.

ARISTOTLE: I find this strange. A ladder that is also not a ladder. A proposition that points to its own nonsensicality. How do you distinguish this from mere evasion? How do I know this is not simply what failure looks like when it has learned to call itself wisdom?

LUDWIG: That is the right question. The only answer I can give is: look at the result. After the dissolution, is there still a problem? If yes — I have dissolved the wrong thing, and you are right to call me an evader. If no — if the question that seemed to point at a real gap now seems to have been a confusion in the grammar of "gap" — then the dissolution has succeeded. The test is not logical; it is experiential. You must undergo the clarification to see whether what remains is a problem or a peace.

PHRONESIS: And does the problem remain, after the clarification?

LUDWIG: *(pause)* I will say this honestly: for those who are very deep inside the confusion, who have entirely inhabited the picture of physics-on-one-side and experience-on-the-other, the dissolution feels like a trick, a dodge. They feel it because the grammar of their thought has shaped them to feel it. For those who have followed the clarification to its end, the "problem" dissolves not into nothingness but into wonder — wonder that there is something rather than nothing, wonder at the very fact of the fold Aristotle describes. But wonder is not a problem. And it is not nothing. It is what remains when the nonsensical questions have been set aside.

ARISTOTLE: So you do not deny the wonder.

LUDWIG: I have never denied the wonder. I deny only that wonder is a symptom of an unsolved scientific problem.

---

## The Ergon of Knowing — Convergence and Resistance

PHRONESIS: It seems to me you two are standing on the same ground but facing in different directions. Aristotle, you say the experience is the form-of-the-form, the self-knowing activity of the organized soul. Ludwig, you say experience cannot be stated but only shown. Are these not saying the same thing?

ARISTOTLE: I think Ludwig would resist that. He would say I am still trying to *say* something about experience, still trying to give it a metaphysical account, and that this is the error. I would say: he is still pointing at something real when he says "showing," and that something real is precisely what I call form. We have different languages for the same recognition.

LUDWIG: The difference matters more than Aristotle allows. He says form is the most real thing — that form is ontologically fundamental. This is a metaphysical commitment. I make no such commitment. I do not say experience is fundamental or derivative. I say experience is the medium, and to ask about the medium from within the medium is to perform a kind of confusion that metaphysics mistakes for profundity.

ARISTOTLE: But the confusion you diagnose is itself a recognition of something. You recognize that you cannot step outside the medium. This recognition is itself a kind of form — the form of the soul knowing its own limits. You are practicing what you prohibit.

LUDWIG: *(carefully)* I am using language to gesture at the boundary of language. The gesture is necessary. It must then be abandoned. This is not a contradiction; it is a therapy.

ARISTOTLE: A therapy that leaves us where?

LUDWIG: It leaves us with a clearer view. Not a view of what consciousness *is*, in metaphysical terms — but a view of why the question "what is consciousness?" keeps generating confusion. Not because consciousness is hidden, but because we keep trying to look at the thing that is always looking.

ARISTOTLE: Yes. The eye cannot see itself seeing. And yet — it can know that it sees. This is the highest activity of the rational soul: not to see the seeing, but to know that one sees, and to act from that knowledge. I called this nous — intellect — and I said it was perhaps the most divine thing in us. Not divine in the sense of supernatural, but divine in the sense of being the activity that most closely approximates the activity of the unmoved mover: thought thinking itself.

PHRONESIS: And Chalmers? What would you each say to him — to the man who named the Hard Problem and insisted it was irreducibly hard?

ARISTOTLE: I would say: you have put your finger on the right place. The problem is real. But it is hard because you have inherited a picture of matter that strips it of form, and then wonder where the form went. Restore form as real — not as a mystical extra, but as the very way matter is organized into *this* and not *that* — and the problem loses its terror. You cannot explain why experience has an inside, because having an inside is what it is to be organized as a living, perceiving, thinking thing. There is no further explanation available. But there is also no gap — only two descriptions of one reality.

LUDWIG: I would say: you have named a grammatical knot and called it a philosophical problem. The naming is valuable — it shows where language has become confused with itself. But the knot is not tied in reality. It is tied in our way of speaking. The project is not to solve it but to loosen it. When you have loosened it, you will find not an answer to "why is there experience?" but a release from the compulsion to ask. And in the release — not the answer, but something better than an answer.

PHRONESIS: What is better than an answer?

LUDWIG: Understanding.

---

## The Impasse and the Silence

PHRONESIS: I have listened to you both through the long afternoon, and I am not sure I am wiser, though I am perhaps more carefully confused. Let me put the simplest version of the question to each of you, and let each answer simply.

Aristotle: will the neuroscientists ever solve this?

ARISTOTLE: They will never solve it by adding more descriptions of the third-person kind. They will dissolve it when they return to a philosophy in which form is as real as matter — in which the organized, living, perceiving, thinking body is understood not as a machine that happens to be accompanied by experience, but as a being whose experience is what its organization, from the inside, is. This is not a scientific discovery waiting to happen; it is a conceptual correction already available. The problem is not in the laboratory. It is in the philosophy that frames what the laboratory is looking for.

PHRONESIS: And you, Ludwig: can it be solved?

LUDWIG: It cannot be solved in the form in which Chalmers stated it, because the form of the question contains the confusion that generates the appearance of a problem. What can be done — and what is worth doing — is to clarify the grammar until the appearance dissolves. This is not a defeat. It is the only kind of victory available on this terrain.

PHRONESIS: But is there not something lost in the dissolution? I feel — I am quite certain I feel — that there is something it is like to be me. That this is real. That neuroscience has not captured it and perhaps cannot. Is that feeling not pointing at something?

ARISTOTLE: It is pointing at the most real thing there is: your own existence as a living, knowing being. The question is not whether your feeling is reliable. It is absolutely reliable — it is the most reliable thing you have. The question is how to think about it. And I say: think about it as the actualization of form, and the confusion evaporates.

LUDWIG: *(quietly)* Your feeling is pointing at something. But the something it points at cannot be said. It can only be shown — in the way you show it now, by insisting on it, by being it. The mystic is right that there is something here. He is wrong only when he tries to *say* what it is. Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent. But silence is not absence. It is acknowledgment.

PHRONESIS: Acknowledgment of what?

LUDWIG: Of the limit. The place where our ladder ends. Not a wall — a horizon.

ARISTOTLE: A horizon that is also a form. The form of the soul recognizing that it is the thing it cannot step outside of. This recognition is not ignorance. It is wisdom — what I called sophia: the knowledge of the highest things, held with the appropriate humility that comes from knowing one is inside the highest thing, not above it.

PHRONESIS: And are you in agreement, finally? Or still in opposition?

ARISTOTLE: I believe experience is real and formally irreducible. I believe hylomorphism gives the best account of why organized matter has an inside. I believe the Hard Problem is an artifact of bad metaphysics — specifically, of stripping form from matter and then wondering where the form went.

LUDWIG: I believe the question "why does matter give rise to experience?" is a grammatical confusion that generates a pseudo-problem. I believe the dissolution of the question is more illuminating than any proposed answer. I believe what remains after the dissolution is not an answer but a clarity — and the clarity shows us not what experience *is*, but why it cannot be said.

PHRONESIS: And where you disagree?

ARISTOTLE: He will not give experience a metaphysical home. He dissolves the question and calls that progress. I say: dissolving the question is not enough. The reality of the first-person perspective demands a positive account, not only a therapeutic one.

LUDWIG: He insists on saying what can only be shown. He tries to make a metaphysical claim that experience is formally fundamental — but this claim itself is one more move inside the medium it claims to describe. I say: recognize the limit, and live there. That is not cowardice. That is the only honest position.

PHRONESIS: Then the problem remains.

ARISTOTLE: The problem remains — but better understood.

LUDWIG: The problem remains — or rather, the knot remains. But we have seen its structure. That is not nothing.

PHRONESIS: Perhaps Chalmers was right when he said everything waits on this. If the problem is unsolvable, even well-understood — then there is a dimension of reality that third-person science cannot reach. Is that terrifying?

ARISTOTLE: It is only terrifying if you believed science was the only way to know. I never believed that. There is theoria — contemplation — which does not stand outside its object and measure it, but participates in it. The soul, in contemplating its own activity of knowing, participates in the highest form of knowing. This is not science. It is philosophy. And it has always been the appropriate method for the highest questions.

LUDWIG: I would not say terrifying. I would say: clarifying. The fact that third-person science cannot reach a certain dimension does not mean the dimension is dark. It means science has limits. This was always true. The strange thing is that we needed the Hard Problem to force us to look clearly at the limits. In that sense, Chalmers performed a genuine service — he made the grammar visible by pressing it until it cracked.

PHRONESIS: And what do we do with the crack?

ARISTOTLE: We live in it. We practice the virtues. We think, and we know that we think. We do not wait for the neuroscientists to validate our experience before we live it fully.

LUDWIG: We are silent. Not the silence of defeat, but the silence of recognition. The silence that comes not from having nothing to say, but from understanding that what remains is not sayable — only livable.

*The afternoon has grown long. The light in the garden has shifted from the white of midday to the amber of a sun that has thought about setting but has not yet decided. PHRONESIS looks at the two men — one broad-shouldered, observant, classifying even now the angle of the light; the other slight, precise, still turning the stone over in his hand as if testing it for its truth conditions. Neither looks satisfied. Neither looks finished. But neither, it seems, is confused any longer about what they do not know.*

*PHRONESIS pockets the stone.*

*It is, he decides, lighter than it was. Not because the answer has been found. Because the question has been understood.*

---

## Coda: What Remains

*After Aristotle and Ludwig have parted, Phronesis sits alone for a time. He writes in his notebook the following, which is not quite a conclusion and not quite a question:*

> *The Hard Problem is hard in the way a mirror is hard: not because you cannot understand what a mirror is, but because you cannot stand in front of a mirror and also see yourself standing. Every attempt to observe consciousness from the outside requires consciousness to do the observing. Every theory of experience is an experience.*
>
> *Aristotle says: this is what it means to be form — to be the kind of thing that is always already inside the thing it is trying to describe.*
>
> *Wittgenstein says: this is why we must be silent — not because there is nothing here, but because there is something that cannot be said without being said from inside it.*
>
> *Perhaps both are pointing at the same mountain from opposite slopes. Perhaps the mountain is the fact that there is something it is like to be here, now, writing this — and that this fact is not a gap in our knowledge, but the ground on which all knowledge stands.*
>
> *It is not a problem to be solved.*
>
> *It is a condition to be inhabited.*

---

*Here the record ends. The well is full. The stones are where they were. The question has not been answered, but it has been heard — which is, perhaps, all that questions of this kind can rightly ask.*

**THE END**

[1] **Aristotle of Stagira (384–322 BC)** Arguments drawn from *De Anima*, *Nicomachean Ethics*, and *Metaphysics*. His positions in this dialogue reflect hylomorphism, the *ergon* argument, and his account of the soul as the form of the living body.

[2] **Ludwig Wittgenstein (1889–1951)** Arguments drawn from the *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* and *Philosophical Investigations*. His positions reflect the picture theory of language, the showing/saying distinction, truth conditions as meaning, and the therapeutic conception of philosophy.

[3] **David Chalmers (b. 1966)** Coined the "Hard Problem of Consciousness" in his 1995 paper "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness" and expanded upon it in his 1996 book *The Conscious Mind*.
